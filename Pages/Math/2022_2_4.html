Finding a functional's extrema with the Euler Lagrange Equation.



Define \(\mathbf{D} := \{y \in C^1[0,1]: y(0) = 1,\ y(\frac{\pi}{6}) = \frac{3}{2}\}\). Find the possible extrema of \(J: \mathbf{D} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), where \(J\) is defined by \(J(y) := \int_0^\frac{\pi}{6}sec^2 y'^3\).

\(Solution.\) First we recognize the Lagrangian: \[L(x,y(x),y'(x)) = \sec^2(x) y'^3(x)\] Calculate the necessary identities: \[\begin{align*} L_y(x,y(x),y'(x)) & = 0\\ L_{y'}(x,y(x),y'(x)) & = 3\sec^2(x)y'^2(x)\\ \frac{d}{dx}L_{y'}(x,y(x),y'(x)) & = 6 \sec^2(x) y'(x) (y''(x) + \tan(x) y'(x)) \end{align*}\] Apply the \(\textit{Euler-Lagrange Equation}\):\[ \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}L_{y'}(x,y(x),y'(x)) & = L_y(x,y(x),y'(x))\\ 6 \sec^2(x) y'(x) (y''(x) + \tan(x) y'(x)) & =0\\ \end{align*}\] We have solution: \[\begin{align*} y(x) &= A \sin(x) +B\\ \end{align*}\] Substitute in the boundary conditions, we get: \[1 = A\sin(0) + B\] \[\frac{3}{2} = A\sin(\frac{\pi}{6}) + B\] And we find: \[A=B=1\] So, the functional \(J(y)\) has a single possible extrema: \[y(x)=\sin(x) + 1\]
