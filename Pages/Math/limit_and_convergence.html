Limit and convergence.



We say the limit of a sequence exists for \(a_n: \mathbb{N} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{A},\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall n \in \mathbb{N}: N < n \Rightarrow |a_n - L| < \epsilon\] then the limit of \(a_n\) exists, or \(a_n\) is convergent, and (the two notations are interchangable) \[\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n = L\] \[n \rightarrow \infty \Rightarrow a_n \rightarrow L\] If we have \(a_n: \mathbb{N} \rightarrow C[a,b]\), or equivalently \(f_n(x)\) is a sequence of functions, we say \(f_n(x)\) is pointwise convergent iff: \[\forall x \in [a,b] \exist L \in \mathbf{A},\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall n \in \mathbb{N}: N < n \Rightarrow |f_n(x) - L(x)| < \epsilon\] We say \(f_n(x)\) isuniformly convergent iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{A},\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall x \in [a,b], \forall n \in \mathbb{N}: N < n \Rightarrow |f_n(x) - L(x)| < \epsilon\] We say the limit of a function exists for \(f: \mathbf{X} \rightarrow \mathbf{Y}\) at \(x_0\) (can be \(\infty\)) iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{Y},\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists \delta > 0,\ \forall x \in X: |x - x_0| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - L| < \epsilon\] then the limit of \(f\) at \(x_0\) exists and (the two notations are interchangable) \[\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0} f(x) = L\] \[x \rightarrow x_0 \Rightarrow f(x) \rightarrow L\] Created May 5th, 2022.
