Limit and convergence.



We say the limit of a sequence exists for \(a_n: \mathbb{N} \rightarrow \mathbf{A}\) iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{A},\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall n \in \mathbb{N}: N < n \Rightarrow d(a_n, L) < \epsilon\] then the limit of \(a_n\) exists, or \(a_n\) is convergent, (or point-wise convergent if \(A\) is function space) and (the two notations are interchangable) \[\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n = L\] \[n \rightarrow \infty \Rightarrow a_n \rightarrow L\] If \(\mathbf{A}\) is function space, or equivalently \(f_n(x)\) is a sequence of functions defined on \([a,b]\), we say \(f_n(x)\) is uniformly convergent iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{A}, \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f_n(x) - L| = 0\] We say the limit of a function exists for \(f: \mathbf{X} \rightarrow \mathbf{Y}\) at \(x_0\) (can be \(\infty\)) iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{Y},\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists \delta > 0,\ \forall x \in X: d(x,x_0) < \delta \Rightarrow d(f(x), L) < \epsilon\] then the limit of \(f\) at \(x_0\) exists and (the two notations are interchangable) \[\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0} f(x) = L\] \[x \rightarrow x_0 \Rightarrow f(x) \rightarrow L\] Created May 5th, 2022.
