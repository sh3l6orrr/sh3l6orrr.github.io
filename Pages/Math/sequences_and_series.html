Sequences and series.



Definition
Sequences are functions \(a: \mathbb{N} \rightarrow \mathbf{M}\).
If a sequence takes the form \(a_n = \sum_{0}^n b_k\), where \(b_k\) is a sequence, then it's called a series.

Convergence
Recall that a sequence defined in \(\mathbf{Y}\) is convergent iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{Y}:\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n = L\] A series is said to converge if its partial sum's sequence converge: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{Y}:\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sum_0^n b_k = L\]

P-series: \(\sum_{0}^\infty (\frac{1}{k})^p\).

  Harmonic series:\(\sum_{0}^\infty \frac{1}{k}\).

Power series: \(\sum_{0}^\infty c_k(x-c)^k\).

  Geometric series: \(\sum_{0}^\infty x^k\).

  Maclaurin series: \(\sum_{0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}}{k!}(0)x^k\).

  Taylor series: \(\sum_{0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}}{k!}(c)(x-c)^k\).

Generalized Fourier series: \(\sum_{0}^\infty \langle f(x), \phi_k \rangle \phi_k\).

  Complex Fourier series: \(\sum_{-\infty}^\infty \langle f(x), e^{ikx} \rangle e^{ikx}\).

  Fourier series: \(\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{1}^\infty \langle f(x), \cos(kx) \rangle \cos(kx) + \langle f(x), \sin(kx) \rangle \sin(kx)\).

    Fourier cosine series: \(\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{1}^\infty \langle f(x), \cos(kx) \rangle \cos(kx)\).

    Fourier sine series: \(\sum_{1}^\infty \langle f(x), \sin(kx) \rangle \sin(kx)\).


Cauchy sequence
There's a different way to derive convergence of sequences. A sequence is called Cauchy iff \[\forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall m,n > N: d(a_m, a_n) < \epsilon\] We define complete metric spaces to be those in which all Cauchy sequences converge. Common compete metric spaces include Euclidean space, Banach space, etc. Therefore in those spaces we can show a sequence is Cauchy and is thus convergent.
Multiplication of sequences
Product of sequences: \[\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} b_n = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n b_n\] Product of series is also called the Cauchy product: \[\sum_0^{\infty}a_k\sum_0^{\infty}b_k = \sum_0^{\infty}\sum_0^k a_l b_{k-l}\]
