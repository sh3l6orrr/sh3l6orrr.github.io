Sequences and series.



Definition

Sequences are functions with a domain of the naturals. If a sequence takes the form \(a_n = \sum_{0}^n b_k\), where \(b_k\) is a sequence, then it's called a series.

Common types of series are power series (incl. MacLaurin Series, Taylor Series), p-series (incl. Reimann zeta functions, harmonic series), and generalized Fourier series (incl. Fourier Series, Fourier sin/cosine series).

Convergence

Recall that a sequence defined in \(\mathbf{Y}\) is convergent iff: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{Y}:\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n = L\] A series is said to converge if its partial sum's sequence converge: \[\exist L \in \mathbf{Y}:\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sum_0^n b_k = L\] There's a different way to derive convergence of sequences. A sequence is called Cauchy iff \[\forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall m,n > N: d(a_m, a_n) < \epsilon\] We define complete metric spaces to be those in which all Cauchy sequences converge. Common compete metric spaces include Euclidean space, Banach space, etc. Therefore in those spaces we can show a sequence is Cauchy and is thus convergent.

Arithmetic

Product of sequences: \[\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} b_n = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n b_n\] Product of series is also called the Cauchy product: \[\sum_0^{\infty}a_k\sum_0^{\infty}b_k = \sum_0^{\infty}\sum_0^k a_l b_{k-l}\]

Analyticity

Functions that can be written in the form of power series are called analytic: \[f(x) = \sum_0^{\infty} c_k x^k\] Power series are known to converge uniformly within their interval of convergence. By the fact that the derivative of a power series is also a power series and thus also converge uniformly, analytic functions are thus infinitely differentiable.
