Limit, convergence, and continuity.



We say the limit of a sequence exists for \(a_n\) and the limit is \(L\) iff: \[\exist L ,\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}, \forall n \in \mathbb{N}: N < n \Rightarrow |a_n - L| < \epsilon\] and notate \[\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n = L\] We say the limit of a function exists for \(f(x)\) defined on \([a,b]\) at \(x_0\) (can be \(\infty\)) and the limit is \(L\) iff: \[\exist L,\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists \delta > 0,\ \forall x \in [a,b]: |x - x_0| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - L| < \epsilon\] and notate \[\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0} f(x) = L\]
We call a sequence convergent iff: \[\exists L: \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n = L\] We call a sequence of functions defined on \([a,b]\) pointwise convergent iff: \[\exists f(x): \forall x \in [a,b], \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} f_n(x) = f(x)\] We call a sequence of functions defined on \([a,b]\) uniformly convergent iff: \[\exists f(x): \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sup_{x \in [a,b]}|f_n(x) - f(x)| = 0\]
We call a function defined on \([a,b]\) continuous at a point at \(x_0 \in [a,b]\) iff: \[\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0} f(x) = f(x_0)\] We call a function defined on \([a,b]\) continuous iff: \[\forall x_0 \in [a,b]: \lim_{x\rightarrow x_0} f(x) = f(x_0)\] Uniform continuity cannot, however, be stated in this fashion, so we have to use the \(\epsilon -\delta\) formulation. We call a function defined on \([a,b]\) uniformly continuous iff: \[\forall p,q \in [a,b],\ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists \delta > 0: |p - q| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(p) - f(q)| < \epsilon\]
