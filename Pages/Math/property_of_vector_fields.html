Property of vector fields.



Helmholtz decomposition

Any vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) can be expressed as below, where \(\Phi\) is the scalar potential and \(\mathbf{A}\) is the vector potential: \[\mathbf{F} = - \nabla \Phi + \nabla \times \mathbf{A}\]

Solenoidal field

A vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) is said to be solenoidal (incompressible) if: \[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0\] So its helmholtz decomposition is: \[\mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}\]

Irrotational field

A vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) is said to be irrotational if: \[\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 0\] So its helmholtz decomposition is: \[\mathbf{F} = - \nabla \Phi \] An irrotational vector field is conservative if it's domain is simply connected.

Laplaician field

If both of the above two criterions are satisfied, i.e. \(\mathbf{F}\) is both solenoidal and irrotational, then it is said to be Laplacian, satisfying: \[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 0\] And it can be helmholtz decomposed two ways: \[\mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A} = - \nabla \Phi \] We can observe that the Laplacian vector field satisfies: \[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \nabla \cdot (- \nabla \Phi) = - \nabla^2 \Phi = -\Delta \Phi = 0\] that is, its scalar potential satisfies the Laplace equation and is therefore a harmonic function.
