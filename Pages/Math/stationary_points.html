Stationary points.



Definition

The stationary points a functional \(J: \mathbf{M} \rightarrow \mathbf{R}\), where \(\mathbf{M}\) is a manifold of funtions, satisfies the necessary condition \[\forall v \in \mathbf{M}: \delta J[y;v] = 0\] where \(\delta J[y;v]\) is the Gateaux variation, i.e. the directional derivative of \(J\).

Necessary condition

Based on this identity we can derive the Euler-Lagrange Equation that gives all stationary points of a functional of the form \(J(y) := \int_a^b L(x,y,y') dx\): \[\frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial y}\]

Examples

Example 1. Define \(\mathbf{D} := \{y \in C[0,1]: y(0) = 1,\ y(\frac{\pi}{6}) = \frac{3}{2}\}\). Find the stationary points of \(J: \mathbf{D} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), where \(J\) is defined by \(J(y) := \int_0^\frac{\pi}{6}sec^2 y'^3\).

\(Solution.\) First we recognize the Lagrangian: \[L(x,y(x),y'(x)) = \sec^2(x) y'^3(x)\] Calculate the necessary identities: \[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} & = 0\\ \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} & = 3\sec^2(x)y'^2(x)\\ \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} & = 6 \sec^2(x) y'(x) (y''(x) + \tan(x) y'(x)) \end{align*}\] Apply the Euler-Lagrange Equation:\[ \begin{align*} 6 \sec^2(x) y'(x) (y''(x) + \tan(x) y'(x)) & =0\\ \end{align*}\] We have solution: \[\begin{align*} y(x) &= A \sin(x) +B\\ \end{align*}\] Substitute in the boundary conditions, we get: \[1 = A\sin(0) + B\] \[\frac{3}{2} = A\sin(\frac{\pi}{6}) + B\] And we find: \[A=B=1\] So, the functional \(J(y)\) has a single stationary point: \[y(x)=\sin(x) + 1\]
Example 2. Let \(J: C[0,1] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be defined by \(J(y) = 2y^3(0) - 3y^2(0)\), where \(C[0,1]\) is a normed space with \(\lVert \cdot \rVert_M = \max\lvert y \rvert\). Prove \(y_0(x) = 1\) is a local minimum of \(J\).

\(Proof.\) Assume \(y \in S_1(y_0)\). Then \(\lVert y - y_0 \rVert < 1\). By definition, \(\max\lvert(y - y_0)(x)\rvert < 1 \), which implies \(\lvert y(x) - 1\rvert < 1 \). Then by replacing the absolute value, \[0 < y < 2 \] Using this inequality, we show \(J(y) - J(y_0) \geqslant 0\): \[ \begin{aligned} J(y) - J(y_0) &=2y^3(0) - 3 y^2(0) - 2y_0^3(0) + 3 y_0^2(0) \\ &=2y^3(0) - 3 y^2(0) + 1 \\ & \geqslant \min (2y^3 - 3y^2) - 1 \\ &=-1 + 1\\ &=0 \end{aligned}\] Therefore, \(\forall y \in S_1(y_0): J(y) \geqslant J(y_0)\). We've proved \(y_0(x)=1\) is a local minimum for \(J\) as desired. \(Q.E.D.\)
