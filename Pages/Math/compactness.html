Compactness.



Definition

A set is called compact iff each of its open cover contains a finite subcover.

General Properties

Compactness of a set does not depend on its embedding space, unlike open sets or closed sets. Therefore, it makes sense to talk about a compact space.

Compactness is equivalent to completeness and total-boundedness. Since complete sets are closed and total-bounded sets are bounded, compact sets are also closed and bouneded (The converse is not guaranteed, but true in \(\mathbb{R}^n)\).

A closed subset of a compact space is compact.

Properties in metric spaces

All sequences of a compact set admit a convergent subsequence, i.e. sequentially compact.

As an example, the Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem states: a closed and bounded set in Euclidean space is sequentially compact.

A sequence of nonempty compact sets \(K_n\) such that \(K_{n+1} \subseteq K_n\) has nonempty intersection, i.e. limit point compact: \[\bigcup_0^{\infty}K_n \neq \varnothing\]
